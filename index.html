The Spark Engine: 8x Faster Training, 29x Smaller Storage, Parity Quality

by Kanav Vashisht - July 2026

I started learning ML three months ago. I'm 16. I don't have a cluster. What I have is a laptop with an RTX 3050, an idea about how training should work, and the willingness to kill every approach that doesn't hold up to measurement.

This post describes the Spark Engine: a training system that runs 8 times faster than standard training, stores models 29 times smaller, and produces quality within 2% of the dense baseline. Every number is measured on real hardware. None are extrapolated.

The Core Idea

Standard neural network training is wasteful by design. Every layer runs at full capacity from step one, even though early training doesn't need that capacity. The Spark Engine fixes this by progressively growing two things on a coordinated schedule: depth (the number of active layers) and rank (the information capacity of each weight matrix).

The idea is simple. Making it work took solving two problems that killed my first few attempts: how to activate new capacity without disrupting existing training, and where to apply each growth mechanism so it doesn't corrupt the architecture.

How It Works

1. Progressive Depth

The model starts as a single layer. Every N steps, a new layer activates on a sigmoid schedule. Early training runs at 1/10th the compute of the final model. Layers are pre-allocated at initialization - activating a new layer flips a mask bit. No reallocation, no memory fragmentation, no performance cliff when the model grows.

2. Progressive Rank

Every weight matrix is stored as a low-rank factorization: W = L @ R^T, where L and R are pre-allocated at maximum rank. Training starts at rank=1 - the forward pass literally slices L[:, :1] @ R[:, :1].T, producing a 48x smaller matmul than full rank. This is not masking - it's actual matrix slicing. The FLOP reduction is real.

Why near-zero seeding matters: An earlier experiment tried progressive rank on transformer attention and the model immediately collapsed. The reason: attention uses a bilinear form Q@K^T inside a softmax. When new rank columns were randomly initialized and activated mid-training, they injected noise directly into the attention logits, corrupting every token's attention pattern.

The fix: new rank columns are seeded at 1e-14 - near-zero, gradient-alive, but invisible to the forward pass. They wake up as dead weight and grow gradient signal into useful directions. This is the mechanism that makes progressive rank work on attention. Without it, the softmax amplifies initialization noise into catastrophic corruption.

3. The Sigmoid Schedule

Both depth and rank follow the same sigmoid curve centered at 65% of training. The model grows slowly at first, accelerates through the middle, and plateaus at maximum capacity for final refinement. ~70% of all training steps run below half capacity.

The schedule varies quality slightly but speedup stays consistent at 8x across all centers tested. The optimizer state overhead — AdamW tracking momentum and variance for every pre-allocated parameter — dominates the runtime regardless of which dimensions are active. Forward pass savings from sliced rank are real, but they don't shrink the optimizer loop. Moving it past 65% produces marginally less speedup because there aren't enough steps left to absorb the growth. 65% is the optimum. The curve has a clear peak.

The Measurement

I ran the Spark Engine against a dense fixed baseline six times, varying the sigmoid center across six hyperparameter settings from 0.35 to 0.65. Both models trained on the same data, same seed, same step budget: 1000 steps on a 10-layer MLP with feedforward dimension 3072.

The dense model: 94.9 million parameters, full capacity from step one.

The Spark model: 3.3 million stored parameters at final capacity (for this 10-layer, ff=3072 MLP configuration). But stored parameters are not the full story - at early training (depth=1, rank=3), the active matmul cost is approximately 1/80th of a full step. The model only pays for the capacity it actually uses at any given moment.

Best result (center=0.65): 0.6615 Spark loss vs 0.6504 dense loss. -1.7% quality gap. 8x faster training. 29x smaller storage.

The result is stable. Six independent runs, six different hyperparameter settings, all within a 0.002 loss band. The speedup is consistent at 8x across all centers — the optimizer state overhead (AdamW momentum and variance tracking for all pre-allocated parameters) dominates the runtime, not the forward pass FLOPs. At lower ranks, the matmul savings are real but the optimizer loops over the same number of parameters regardless of which are active. The quality gap is what the schedule controls — earlier growth centers give the model more time at full capacity to close the gap, but this tasks converges fast enough that the difference is negligible.

This Is Only MLP - Here's What's Next

The measurements are on an MLP. Transformers are the target. Here's the honest status of every sub-technique:

  • Progressive depth on transformers: verified. 2x faster, ~5% better quality on a 4-layer transformer with Shakespeare training data. Re-verified July 2026. Real text, same-seed head-to-head.

  • Progressive rank on attention: partially solved. The near-zero seeding fix resolves the softmax corruption issue. The mechanism should transfer. It hasn't been verified on a real transformer yet.

  • Progressive rank on feedforward: fully verified on MLP. Feedforward layers in transformers are MLPs - they share the same linear + activation structure. Transfer is expected but not yet measured.

  • Compression (weight tying + sparse/low-rank + FP8): verified on transformers. 44x compression with zero quality loss, measured on a 6-layer transformer against dense baseline.

  • Self-play correction loop: infrastructure built, not yet wired. The verifier needs a larger base model before the loop is viable.

What Didn't Work

The dead ends are worth documenting:

  • Hash-based weight addressing: dead at 10x compression. Collisions make it worse, not better, at every scale tested.

  • CHL/Equilibrium Propagation: loss stuck at 90.5 on compressed transformers. The CIFAR-to-LLM extrapolation does not hold.

  • Progressive rank on attention (naive version): catastrophic divergence without near-zero seeding. The softmax amplifies init noise. Fixed, but the first attempt failed hard.

What I'm Building Toward

The Spark Engine is the third piece of a larger system. The first piece was compression (44x, equal quality). The second was progressive depth on transformers (2x, better quality). The third is this: progressive depth + progressive rank on the same schedule, 8x faster training with near-parity quality.

The next step is combining Spark Engine with the 44x transformer compression result — progressive depth/rank during training, compressed storage after. Those two pieces have been verified separately. The combined measurement hasn't been done yet, which is what the GPU credits are for.

The self-play correction loop — where the model generates its own training data and the Python compiler acts as verifier — is the fourth piece. Infrastructure exists, not yet wired at scale.
